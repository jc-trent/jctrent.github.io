Void-Born · Leviathan

Chapter One

The rock shuddered beneath them like a tired hull.

Vale hung three meters off the tunnel wall, boots locked to a rusted crossbeam, hands coated to the elbow in powdered stone. Her floodlight cut a hard cone through the dust, glinting off the vein running along the hollowed shaft. Cobalt. Deep blue under the gray, stubborn and heavy.

On her shoulder, Apex shifted his weight. Tiny vibrations ticked through her rig as the Slinker’s magnetic feet adjusted to her lean. His little helmet dome caught the light. It was a battered half-sphere of repurposed visor material, sealed to his neck ridge with the same adhesive she used on EVA frame joints. Through the transparent shell, his lidless eyes tracked dust drifting through the shaft.

“Two meters of dead rock, then the good stuff,” Vale said into the open channel.

“Copy.” Harken’s voice crackled in her ear. “Quit sweet-talking the ore and tell me if we can anchor charges.”

Vale shifted her weight, unlatched one boot, and stepped sideways along the beam. Careful. In null-g the move looked easy, but a bad angle against the crossbeam or an uncontrolled swing into the wall meant a fracture, and a fracture meant four months on the bone clock. Four months of calcium supplements and reduced load while the break knit itself back together without gravity to help. Four months of not earning. She’d seen it happen to better scrappers than her.

She was twenty-four. Void-born kids who didn’t reach Tier 2 started losing bone density in their late twenties. By thirty, the joint pain became chronic. By thirty-five, the fractures started. Forty was optimistic. She’d run the full projection once. The number had not been cheerful. She’d deleted it and moved on with her life, such as it was.

The bone clock. Every void-born spacer had one. Most tried not to think about it. Vale thought about it every time she checked her stats. Check the number, note the trend, get back to work. Worrying about your skeleton dissolving was generally considered unproductive. Either she’d get enough money to fix it, and the worry would be for nothing. Or she wouldn’t, and she’d have plenty of time to worry from a bed.

The mag-seal released with a muted thud. Her body swung on the remaining boot in a slow, controlled arc, long legs knifing through the vacuum. She caught a welded brace with her free hand, pulled herself in until her helmet almost brushed the rough-cut wall, and tapped her ring against it.

The magnetic band around her middle finger was scarred from twelve years of use. It pulled toward the bolts sunk into the vein. A familiar tug. It gave nothing against the raw stone. Ferrous here, but not there. The kind of truth you could feel through a glove.

“Bolts are steel,” she said. “Bulkhead braces behind them too. Good anchorage.”

“Flag the spots and move. We’re burning margin.”

Always burning margin. Six months on the Rusty Lung and Vale had learned that margin was the ship’s native currency. The gap between operational and dead. Every job started with margin and ended with less of it. If what you pulled from the rock was worth more than what you burned getting there, you called it a win.

She tagged three points on her lace with a thought. Tiny blue icons bloomed on the shared overlay.

Below her, the others worked by the cold glow of their own lights. Ysobel and Grix floated near the far end of the tunnel, moving around the partially exposed seam. The cutting rig clung to the wall like a mechanical insect, articulated arms chewing through stone in slow arcs. Dust and rock chips drifted from the cutter’s path.

Closer to the docking collar, Jace guided an empty sled through the bottleneck where the shaft narrowed. His trajectory was fine. His body didn’t believe it yet. Too much grip on the guide rails. Shoulders braced for a fall that couldn’t happen without gravity.

She watched him clear the bottleneck and nudged a course correction through her lace — a gentle ping on his overlay, shifting his approach angle two degrees to avoid the cable run he hadn’t noticed. He adjusted without comment. They’d been doing this long enough that her corrections didn’t need acknowledgment.

Apex’s helmet dome tapped her shoulder plate as his head rotated, tracking Jace’s movement through the shaft. The triple-axial neck joint let his head spin nearly 360 degrees while his body stayed locked to Vale’s frame. It looked wrong. Every mammalian instinct said so. But it was efficient, which was the only thing Apex cared about.

“Vein’s thickest here,” Ysobel said over comms. Her voice made everything sound like a correction, even when it wasn’t. “The cobalt density drops to non-viable within three meters of the primary seam. If we extend the cut radius beyond that, we’re excavating dead mass for transport.”

“Copy,” Harken said. “We take what pays to move and nothing else.”

Vale toggled her lace to scan the surrounding structure.

[SCAN — ENVIRONMENT]
Structural Integrity: Moderate
Anchor Points: 47 tagged (ferrous)
Hazards: Micro-fracture network detected (NE quadrant)
Recommendation: Avoid concentrated stress loads

The fracture network was old. Decades of thermal cycling. She flagged the worst sections on the shared overlay and kept moving, boots kissing the beam with magnetic sighs. Apex rode the motion without complaint, his prehensile tail curled around her suit’s collar ring for extra stability.

She’d had him since her last year on the rigs. A vendor on Titan’s Belt Station had sold her a half-starved Slinker pup for forty credits and a favor. The favor was fixing the man’s EVA frame, which had been slowly killing him through a misaligned pressure seal. The Slinker had been an afterthought. A bio-engineered pest controller for shipboard life. Too small and strange for anyone who didn’t already live in the spaces between walls.

Three ships, two stations, and one very unpleasant cargo shuttle. Apex had survived all of it. He’d clung to her shoulder with the magnetic stubbornness of a creature that had decided where it belonged.

The helmet had come later. Vale’s own design. Ugly, practical, and exactly functional enough. She’d built it during a two-week layover when she realized that her Slinker’s three-to-five-minute vacuum tolerance was a liability during EVA work. The chitinous plates sealed well enough on their own, but the head was the weak point. So she’d cut down a secondary visor dome, shaped it to fit his neck ridges, and sealed it with frame adhesive. Added a tiny air reservoir, a few hours of oxygen give or take. A bone-conduction speaker pressed against the chitin so he could hear her commands even without atmosphere.

It looked ridiculous. It worked perfectly.

“Charges planted in Section A,” she reported. “Sending layout.”

She’d been working while she drifted. Hands moving through the familiar routine of placing charges against rock where the sensor readouts confirmed the cobalt banded thickest. She’d tapped her ring against the bolts before setting each one. Feeling for the magnetic tug. Trusting it more than the scanner overlay.

Stone lied. Signals bounced and blurred. Steel didn’t fake magnetism.

Blue outlines lit up around her markers in everyone’s lace. Harken’s channel carried a soft approving hum.

“Looks clean. Everyone clear the blast zone and check your tethers. Vale, double-check your anchor points.”

They sounded off one by one. Everyone who’d lost a friend in vacuum counted before the charges blew.

“Firing,” Harken said.

The charges went off in a staggered ripple. A sudden heavy pressure through the stone, then a deep thud that Vale felt in her teeth. Dust exploded outward from the freshly cracked seam. Pebbles, chunks of stone, and powder burst into the shaft in slow motion.

Apex flattened against her shoulder, plates compressing instinctively. His magnetic feet locked harder. Through the dome of his helmet, his eyes were wide and tracking, working out the debris vectors the way he worked out rat movements in the rafters, methodically, without urgency.

Her tether went taut. Her boot seals strained. For a moment, it felt like the whole world tried to peel her off the wall.

She rode it out, grinning behind her visor.

“Section A breached,” Ysobel reported. “Vein exposed. Density readings confirm the initial survey data.”

Through the settling dust, the cobalt glowed dully in Vale’s lamp, a wide, continuous band of blue-gray, now broken into jagged plates ready for the cutter.

“Could’ve told you that without the boom,” Vale said.

“You did,” Grix replied from his position near the cutter. “We just wanted to make sure the rock agreed with you.”

The work fell into rhythm after that.

Cut, collect, haul. The sleds filled slowly, mass calculators marking green thresholds in everyone’s laces. Vale moved between tasks — flagging anchor points, checking seam stability, running material to the sleds when Ysobel and Grix needed an extra set of hands.

She was good at it. Better than good. Twelve years of rig work had trained her body to track multiple objects in motion, to predict where debris would go, to position herself for the catch without getting in the way. Her suit performed flawlessly. The seals she’d adjusted held tight, the mag-plates engaged clean. Every time she pushed off from a surface, the equipment responded the way she expected it to.

And she noticed the same thing in the crew’s suits. The seal that used to stick on Jace’s left shoulder moved freely now. Grix’s boot calibration was holding steady. Ysobel’s helmet display ran without the flicker that had plagued it for months.

Nobody said anything. But Vale saw the difference. The hours she’d spent fitting suits were paying off in small ways. Seconds saved here, frustration avoided there.

She’d done the same work on every ship she’d served on. The Silver Current, the Drifter’s Wake, half a dozen freight haulers. She’d leave the suits better than she found them, and then she’d leave. The crews always asked her to stay. She never did, because staying meant watching the margins shrink until there was nothing left. And Vale needed the margins for something specific.

She checked her lace out of habit, the way she did a dozen times a day. A reflex as automatic as checking her tether tension.

[SCAN — SELF]
TIER: 1.6
BODY: 1.6 | MIND: 1.8 | SPIRIT: 1.7

The same numbers she’d been looking at for six months. Body at 1.6, refusing to budge no matter how many hours she worked in vacuum. Mind and Spirit had ticked up once each in the first two months, when new crew and new challenges pushed her. Then the plateau.

Aether was in everything. The rock, the vacuum, the air she was breathing, the bones that were slowly failing her. Ambient energy, saturating the universe like background radiation. Except background radiation didn’t care if you noticed it, and Aether did. Draw it in, reinforce what was already there, get stronger, live longer, think faster.

That was cultivation. That was the whole game. The only issue was that Aether was diffuse, and you had to convince your body to actually use it. Most people couldn’t get past 1.5 without spending obscene money on special pills, elixirs, or serums. No one got anywhere by sitting around meditating for a decade.

Supposedly, if you got far enough, you could do things that make physics cry quietly in the corner. Vale had never met anyone who could, but she’d seen enough Tier 3s move to know the ceiling was a lot higher than her current floor.

Body, Mind, Spirit. Three axes, three ways the energy made you more. The numbers that told you how much of that ambient everything you’d managed to make yours. And hers had been stuck at 1.6 for six months, because cultivation responded to challenge, and hauling cobalt wasn’t a challenge anymore. It was just work.

She closed the display and caught a chunk of cobalt drifting past the collection net. Guided it in with a tap. Moved to the next one.

Her share of this haul would maybe be three hundred credits after the ship’s cut. Her savings was just over six thousand. The cost of a basic bone-density compound, the kind that might push her Body from 1.6 to 1.7, usually ran around ten thousand credits.

At her current earning rate, she’d need another year to afford even the cheap option. A year of stagnation. A year of her bones getting weaker, more brittle, closer to failure.

“Sled four’s at capacity,” Grix reported. “Cycling it back to the hold.”

“Copy,” Harken said. “Vale, take over his position. Grix, swap to cutting support.”

Vale pushed off from her current anchor and drifted toward the collar junction. One tether releasing, another engaging, her body rotating to align with the new orientation without conscious thought. Apex adjusted on her shoulder, weight shifting, his tail curling and uncurling around a different attachment point.

Grix passed her going the other way. He gave her a brief nod through his visor.

“Suits are running clean,” he said on their private channel. “Whatever you did to the shoulder articulation on Jace’s rig, he’s stopped fighting it.”

“That’s the idea.”

“He ask you to fix it?”

“No. I noticed it binding during his second EVA and adjusted it while he was sleeping.” She paused. “He hadn’t mentioned it because he thought that’s just what suits felt like.”

Grix was silent for a moment, before muttering, “Station people.”

“Lay off. Everyone starts on a station.”

Grix’s channel carried a low snort. “Everyone except you.”

He wasn’t wrong. More than ninety-nine percent of spacers were station-born. Raised in spin gravity, educated in pressurized corridors, shaped by the assumption that down was a constant and air was free. Not enough garden worlds in the settled systems to account for the rest, and even the most committed void communities sent their kids to spin stations for schooling.

Most parents weren’t insane enough to raise a child in micro-g.

Vale’s had been. She’d spent her first sixteen years on extraction rigs in the outer system, where the only gravity came from centrifuges that ran when fuel allowed and didn’t when it didn’t. Her bones had formed in that environment. Long, light, optimized for a world without weight. By the time she’d left the rigs, the damage was structural. Permanent, unless cultivation fixed it.

“Station people,” she agreed, letting the rest go unsaid. Grix knew her history. He didn’t need the details repeated.

From deeper in the shaft, Jace’s voice came over the open channel. “Hey, Vale? The sled’s guidance display is showing a symbol I don’t recognize. Looks like a triangle with a line through it.”

A channel caught a sharp exhale. Grix’s voice cut across it before it became words, flat and immediate. “That’s the mass-balance warning. Means the sled’s loaded unevenly. Redistribute the top layer toward the far end and it’ll clear.” Then, to no one in particular, “Good catch. Unbalanced sled in a narrow shaft is how you crack a helmet.”

Jace redistributed the load. The symbol cleared.

Vale smiled behind her visor. Grix had been doing that since the first week. The flat correction that stopped the behavior without making a scene. He’d given Jace the full speech early on, about asking questions, about thermals for anyone who gave him grief about it. The crew had calibrated accordingly. Nobody mocked Jace for not knowing things. At least, not twice.

She took up position at the collar junction, watching the feed of sleds moving through the passage. The Lung’s cargo hold was filling steadily.

Three hundred credits. Her share. Maybe three-fifty if the cobalt purity ran higher than the initial assay. Add it to her savings. Subtract docking fees, personal supplies, the incidentals of living on a ship where nothing was provided and everything was negotiated. Net gain would maybe be a hundred and fifty credits.

At that rate, she’d have enough for the cheap bone compound in fourteen months. Longer if something broke. Longer if the ship hit a dry spell. Longer if the margins kept shrinking the way they’d been shrinking since she came aboard.

The Lung wasn’t dying. Not yet. But it was bleeding. Slowly, steadily, the way old ships did when maintenance costs started outpacing income. Grix kept the drives running. Harken kept the contracts coming. The crew worked hard and ate thin and nobody complained because complaining cost energy that was better spent working.

Vale could see the trajectory. She’d seen it before, on other ships. The slow slide from “getting by” to “barely surviving” to “selling components to make docking fees.” The Lung was somewhere between the first and second stages. Harken knew it even if she didn’t say it.

Six months. No stat progression. No path to the compounds she needed. A ship that was slowly failing despite a crew that deserved better.

She liked these people. That was the problem. She liked Harken’s dry competence and Grix’s flat honesty and the way Ysobel never said anything she hadn’t already measured twice and even Jace’s gravity-born confusion. They’d accepted her without fanfare. Competence over credentials. She liked that Grix had left a corrected valve adapter on her workbench without a note. She liked that Harken said “might need you” in future tense and meant it.

She didn’t want to leave. But wanting wasn’t a plan, and the bone clock didn’t care about wanting.

“Last load’s clear of the tunnel,” Ysobel said eventually. “Continuing extraction beyond this point exceeds our transport capacity.”

“We stop here.” Harken’s voice left no room for discussion. “Enough to cover docking fees. Maybe more. Pack it in.” A beat. “Grix and Jace, sweep for tool drift. Vale, you’re with me.”

“Copy. Where?”

“Topside. Claim beacon needs a verification check.”

They made their way up the shaft toward the asteroid’s surface. Past the docking collar, through the auxiliary hatch, and then nothing but black and stars.

Vale’s heart rate ticked up. Her hands steadied on the guide rails and her breathing slowed, the way it always did when the void opened around her.

The asteroid’s surface rolled out before them. Rough, pitted, barely lit by the distant sun and the Lung’s running lights. The ship crouched on the far side of the collar, rust-brown and oil-stained, an ugly assemblage of welded modules that somehow still flew. A lattice of steel guide cables ran from the docking infrastructure across the surface, anchored to stakes driven into the rock. The skeleton of previous operations, giving mag-boots something to grip.

Vale clipped her tether to the nearest cable and pushed off. The micro-g was so low it might as well not exist. Without the tether and the guide cables, a careless step would send you on a slow, embarrassing drift toward the stars. She pulled herself along the cable in a controlled glide, boots occasionally kissing the steel stakes for orientation.

She loved it.

Apex’s helmet dome caught the starlight. Through the visor, his eyes swept the surface, tracking movement that didn’t exist yet. His breath fogged the inside of the dome in tiny, rhythmic pulses.

Harken pulled herself along the guide cable ahead of Vale, moving with the efficient hand-over-hand that station-born spacers defaulted to on surfaces. The claim beacon stood where she’d planted it three days ago, bolted to a steel platform near the end of the cable run. Orange paint flaking, ID broadcast pulsing through Vale’s lace.

Harken didn’t speak for a while. She hooked her tether to the platform’s anchor ring and verified the beacon code, checked the broadcast strength, ran through the confirmation sequence. Methodical. She verified each step herself before moving to the next. Vale floated nearby, one hand on the cable, watching the stars, running numbers she already knew the answer to.

“You’re quiet today,” Harken said without looking up from the access panel.

“Thinking.”

“About?”

Fourteen months. That was the number. Fourteen months to afford the cheap compound that would push her Body one rank. Assuming nothing went wrong. Assuming the Lung stayed solvent. Assuming the margins held.

“Math,” Vale said.

Harken’s helmet turned toward her. For a moment, nothing. Her visor reflected the stars and Vale’s own suit and the small dome of Apex’s helmet catching light.

“Bad math?”

“Slow math.”

Harken grunted. She finished the beacon confirmation, unhooked from the panel, and drifted to the end of her tether. She looked out at the asteroid’s surface for a long moment, deciding how much to say.

“You found the seam,” she said. “Under all the dead stone. Your read on the geology saved us two days of wasted cutting.” She paused. “Figured you should see how we close the loop. How the whole operation fits together, start to finish. Might need you to run a beacon check solo someday.”

Harken was making Vale’s departure harder by making her more essential, and she wasn’t being subtle about it. The slow accumulation of responsibility, handed over piece by piece.

“The suits are in the best shape they’ve been in years,” Harken continued. “Grix told me. You haven’t just maintained them. Improved. You’re not following maintenance specs, you’re upgrading by hand. The whole crew’s running smoother because of work you’re doing in your off-hours that nobody asked for.”

“The specs are written for standard suits. The Lung doesn’t have standard suits. It has six different models welded together and praying.”

“Exactly. That’s what I mean. You don’t just fix. You understand.” Harken paused. “And the frame-fitting business at station is pulling real credits.”

It wasn’t enough. Harken knew. Of course she knew. She was a captain, and captains tracked crew retention the way engineers tracked seal integrity. She knew the bone clock. She knew the cost of compounds. She’d done the same math.

“I’m not making you promises I can’t keep,” Harken said. “The Lung can’t afford cultivation support right now. Maybe not for a while. But I need you to know that I’m looking for a way.”

Vale stared at her. It was the most direct thing Harken had said in six months. The situation wasn’t good enough, and she was saying so plainly.

“Thank you, Captain,” Vale said. It wasn’t enough. Nothing was enough for the gap between what she needed and what was available. But it was honest, and on the Lung, honesty had currency.

On her shoulder, Apex’s weight shifted. She felt his mandibles working through the vibration in his magnetic feet. A faint pulse against her suit’s plating. Maybe responding to her posture. Maybe just stretching.

Click. She couldn’t hear it through vacuum. She supplied the sound herself.

Harken’s visor turned toward the Slinker. “That thing is growing on me. Don’t tell anyone I said that.”

“Wouldn’t dream of it.”

They floated for a moment, two figures tethered to a rock in the dark. The beacon’s pulse blinked steady on Vale’s lace. She ran the numbers one more time. Same answer. She watched the orange blink of it and knew she’d carry leaving people like this with her.

They were halfway back along the guide cable when Vale’s lace chimed.

A new contact pinged in the corner of her vision, faint at first, then sharpening as her suit’s passive sensors confirmed it wasn’t a ghost. Something on an approach vector, burning just enough to counter its drift.

“Captain,” Vale said. “You seeing that?”

Harken’s grip on the cable tightened. “Stand by.”

The contact’s details resolved: mass estimate, velocity, projected intercept.

Then the transponder tag flashed up.

IRON NAILS // SECT-CRAFT // INDEPENDENT SALVAGE

“Of course.” Harken’s voice went flat. Her jaw tightened behind the visor, and when she spoke again every word was clipped short. “Inside. Now.”

Vale looked out into the dark. A single star among many, crawling slowly against the fixed pattern. Coming closer.

Apex’s head rotated inside his dome, tracking the distant contact through the visor. His body stayed locked to Vale’s shoulder, feet magnetized. But his attention had shifted. New movement meant new variables.

“How rude do you think they’re going to be?” Vale asked.

“Rude enough that we’re about to earn every credit this beacon represents.” Harken was already hauling herself along the cable toward the collar, hand over hand, fast. “Inside. Now.”

Vale pushed off and followed, pulling herself along the guide cable in long, efficient sweeps. The contact icon on her lace brightened with every second.

The Lung was already coming alive. She could feel it through the cable’s vibration. Systems spinning up, crew moving to stations. Through the docking collar, Grix would be running diagnostics. Ysobel would be pulling sensor data. Jace would be asking what was happening, and someone would be answering.

Whatever came next, she’d deal with it when it arrived. Right now she just needed to get inside before the universe decided she was due for a setback.

That's all for now.
